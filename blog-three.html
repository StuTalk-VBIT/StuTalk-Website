KIT-KAT IN JAPAN

  • Vetha Varshini Kavya Allam
  • Class of 2020

In Japanese, Kit-Kat is Kitto Katto. The Japanese word Kitto can mean “surely”, and the word Katsu is “to win”. Little wordplay, and that would collectively mean “You’re bound to win”.

Now, this just made it easier to understand why almost every student in Japan buys a Kit-Kat before an exam, and how, some even carry it to their tests.

The OH-WOW factor here is that it was totally unintentional. Nestle had no idea that Kit-Kat would strike the Japanese market this way. When they found out about it, Nestle customized Kit-Kats with cheerful messages like “Do your best!”, “It’ll be fine”, and “Believe in yourself!”.

And now that’s what brand flexibility means!

More often than not, we hear about how emotional connectivity helps your brand strike the right chord with your audience.

Because the most impactful connection is emotional. You know it when you feel it! As simple as that.

Later on, Nestle replaced the plastic wrappers with paper ones. This paper packaging included instructions on how to make an origami crane out of it, instead of just throwing it away!

And now that was a brilliant move on two fronts

One, origami, in itself, is from Japan, and it means fold+paper – ori+kami. As for cranes though, they have a mystical appeal in Japan. They’re known to live for about a thousand years and thereby, represent good luck and longevity! Besides, it’s a traditional Japanese belief that if you folded 1,000 origami cranes, your wish will come true.

Two, since Kit-Kat’s already played up the “good luck” charm in its marketing in Japan, now they’re encouraging people to buy Kit-Kat and not throw away the wrapper. Instead, make an origami paper crane out of it, write their wish/message in the wings and give it to people as a lucky charm!

After all, who’d throw away something that came as a part of good luck!

And that’s how Nestle established a wonderful marketing strategy that blent in with their culture, connected emotionally and continues to sale out big in the Japanese market. Quite a case study!
